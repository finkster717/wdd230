Design Principles Document

Michael Fink

Exhibit A

Visual Hierarchy: Visual hierarchy refers to the arrangement or presentation of elements in a way that implies importance. It guides the viewer's eye through a design, highlighting essential elements.

Hick's Law:
Hick's Law states that the time it takes for a person to make a decision is proportional to the number of choices available. In web design, it emphasizes the importance of simplicity and minimizing the number of options presented to users.

Fitt's Law:
Fitt's Law relates to the time it takes to move to a target area, such as a button, based on the size and distance of the target. It's often used to optimize the placement and size of interactive elements for better usability.

Rule of Thirds:
The Rule of Thirds involves dividing an image or design into a grid of nine equal parts, with important elements placed along the grid lines or at their intersections. It's a compositional guideline for creating balanced and visually appealing designs.

White Space and Clean Design:
White space, or negative space, is the area between design elements. It contributes to readability and a clean design by preventing visual clutter. Embracing white space is crucial for a minimalist and user-friendly interface.

PARC: Proximity, Alignment, Repetition, Contrast:
These principles, often referred to as the "PARC" principles, are fundamental in design:
Proximity: Group related elements together to create visual unity.
Alignment: Align elements to establish order and connection.
Repetition: Repeat design elements for consistency and recognition.
Contrast: Use contrast to highlight important elements and create visual interest.
